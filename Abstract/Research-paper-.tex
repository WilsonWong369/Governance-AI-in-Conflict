\section{Main Objectives and Research
Question}\label{main-objectives-and-research-question}

The main objective of this research of the need of development of an
robust hard law on the international stage regarding conflict using
Artificial Intelligence (AI).

Main Research Question: Has the Ukraine--Russia war led international
institutions to change or strengthen their governance frameworks for
military AI?

Hypothesis: After a major security crisis intergovernmental
organizations (UN, NATO) will produce more AI focused governance outputs
(reports, committee mandates, or draft legal texts) in the two years
following the crisis than in the two years before it.

\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has rapidly integrated into our world of
conflict by reshaping how warfare works, targeting systems, and the
deployment of military technology. The 2022 Russia-Ukraine war
highlighted significant challenges regarding both weapons systems and
the legal and governance frameworks governing AI use, raising concerns
about accountability, the rules of war, and the adequacy of current
international regulatory frameworks. Despite the growing usage of AI in
military contexts, there remains limited empirical analysis of whether
international institutions have meaningfully adapted their governance
responses considering this conflict. This study will look towards the
Ukraine-Russia war as a critical juncture in the global governance of
military AI. Drawing on theories such as global governance and
historical institutionalism, this research analyzes how international
institutions respond to major security shocks and technological
disruptions, especially regarding the distinction between hard-law and
soft-law regulatory approaches. The study will use a qualitative
research design based on document and thematic analysis of official
government documents, reports, and statements issued by international
institutions between 2015-2025. It will test the hypothesis that
intergovernmental organizations produce a greater number of AI
governance outputs in the two years after the invasion started. By
examining the content of these outputs, this research assesses whether
institutional responses represent incremental adaptation or more
substantial governance change. The findings aim to contribute to broader
debates on AI governance, global security, and institutional change in
the context of modern warfare.

\subsection{Research Section}\label{research-section}

This study will conduct a qualitative comparative document analysis to
futher understand how international governmental organizations (IGOs)
responded to the governanace of military domain in artifical
intelligence (AI) in the post-invasion of the Russia- Ukraine invasions
(2022). This approach is appropriate because of the research questions
concerns of international behaviour, nromative change and governanace
outputs which puts a phenomena of interpretive analysis rather than
statistical measurement. The document analysis is suitable for this
research because official institutional documents reflect the formal
positions, tone and regulatory commitments of organisations at specific
historical moments. This context of this study, official IGO dcouments
has constitute direct primary evidence of institutional responses to
challenge of military AI governanace, enabling systematic comparison of
outputs before and after the critical date of February 24, 20222.

This selection is to only focus on IGO-level documents for this
empirical literature. As Tallberg et al. (2023) demonstrate is how
international institutions is the primary sites in which how AI
governanace norms are developed and disseminated on the international
stage. Sweijs and Romansky (2024) further contested that IGOs represent
the main arena for the development of international norms on AI
military, making their offical outputs of the most appropriate unit of
analysis for the study concerned with governanace- level change.

These documents sourced from offical institutional archives, government

authenticity and validity in the research. The inclusion criteria

outputs.

The cricial divided is February 24th 2022 as this is the date where the

\subsection{Analytical Framework}\label{analytical-framework}

The study will apply Historical Instituitonalism (HI) as its primary
tehoretical lens. HI assist a the framework to understand how
governanace strctures emerge, envolve and establish for long-term
institutional trajectories shaped by a path-dependent constraints
(Thelen (1999)). As Mahoney and Thelen (2010) establish, during crisis
conditions, instituiotn tend lends towards incremental adaptation rather
than transformative change using existing rules and norms rather adding
or replacing it. This establsihment is the central to the study: the
Russia- Ukraine invasion may have triggered a significant increase in
IGO activites in governanace while stopping well short of biding
hard-law instruments that a full critical juncture would require.

Besides HI, Global Governanace Theory provides a complmentary framework.
Tallberg et al. (2023) argues threats in a transnational context can
generate pressure for a collboration IGO responses, but this responses
frequently remain very fragemented and non-binding in practice. The
forgotten of a unified governnace framework on goverence of military AI
despite greater recongnitions of the risks without shows a broader
pattern of soft-law dominance oberserved across the dataset (Herrera
Pérez (2024); Sweijs and Romansky (2024)).

A central distinction in this research is hard law and soft law
regulatory approachs. Hard law refers to be legally binding instruments:
Treaties, conventions and acts can be enforceable. Soft law is non
bidning, principles, resolutions that carry nromative influces without
any legal obligations nor enforcement. Sweijs and Romansky (2024)
establish a distinction of foundational to evaluating progress in AI
governance in millitary domain, that the current stage of international
landscape is heavily dominated by soft-laws. from the 29 documents in
the datasets provides a empirical basis for understadning whether
post-invasion governanace outputs reflects the substantive change or the
continuation of the current performances and soft-law preferences.

\subsection{Ottawa Treaty Benchmark}\label{ottawa-treaty-benchmark}

This research uses the Ottawa Treaty (1997) it is the Convention on the
Prohibition of the Use, Stockpiling, Production and Transfer of
Anti-personnel Mines and on their Destruction to be a comparative
governanace benchmark. It as been selected becuase it represents a case
of crisis driven into hard law success in international security
governanace. it emerged from crisis to a legally binding obligation with
defined law and achieved near- universal state adoption within a short
period. From the perspective of Historical Institutionalism, this treaty
illustrates a case which permissive conditions were accompanied by
sufficient productve conditions haivng the political will to generate
transformative binding law. (Capoccia and Kelemen (2007);Mahoney and
Thelen (2010)).

The Benchamrks will be appling not only suggest military AI governanace
must follow a 100\% pathway but to allow a measurable and historically
ground standard aginst which current IGO responses can be assessed.
However, this benchmark has fall short on governanace AI in terms of
binding obligation, agreed definitions and paricipation by major AI
powers. This gap establish evidnece that the critical juncture has
produced institutional change but not fully transformation. This
approach is consistent with existing research, which frequently
reference the Ottawa Treaty as a model as a benchmark of its emerging
international security governanace frameworks (Sweijs and Romansky
(2024); Herrera Pérez (2024)).

\section{Limitations}\label{limitations}

There were serveral limitations must be acknowledged. First, the
Russia-Ukraine war remains ongoing, meaning some documents may be
incomplete or subject to revision. As Hendrycks (2025) notes, the rapid
development of AI will creates challenges for governanace research, as
intsituional responses may be lag behind technological relities. Second,
the study only focuses on IGO-levels governanaces and does not examine
States AI defence politics. Finally, the focus on five institutions, UN,
NATO, EU, OECD and COE amy undeprresent governanace activities occuring
in other states or bilateral forums. Despite these limitations, this
study heavily reliance on official primary sources, acedmic artcles and
a clearly oeperationalised theoritecial framework grounded in Historical
Institutionalism and Global Governance Theory provides a rigorous and
reproducible basis for its findings.
